Iomega released these in late 1994, and for its time the disks used were medium to high capacity drives, with space clocking in at 100 megabytes, and being expanded later on to 250 and 750 megabytes. Hard drives at the time were around 500mb in size, making these a relatively cheap way to back a computer up. While the zip drive itself cost $200, the same as the 500mb hard drive, the disks were relatively cheap at around a quarter of the price.

Despite being 3 decades old, they are still used today, in both aeronautical flight control computers, and by vintage tech enthusiasts.

Also pictured is an external zip drive, which could be connected to a PC to use Zip disks.

There was a controversy around a fault in Zip drive hardware dubbed the "Click of Death". The heads that read the Zip disk would be prone to misalignment over time, and eventually would fail completely, making a rhythmic clicking sound as the heads tried to reset their position over and over.

The drive also had a feature which worked on paper like so: if power to the drive was cut unexpectedly, the heads would retract quickly to prevent damage if the disk was pulled out. However the quick motion of the retraction caused the drive to be more likely to fail, and damage the disk in the process, spreading the "Click of Death" to different drives.

Funnily enough, PC World magazine rated it the 15th worst tech product in 2006, and the following year rated it the 23rd best!
